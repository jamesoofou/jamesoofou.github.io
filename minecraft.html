AI to beat Minecraft?


link

We think this is possible but unlikely. By naive extrapolation, video generation models should have a 50% time-horizon of more than a minute by EOY 2026. This is likely not enough, given the complexity of a Minecraft speedrun. But LLM-based systems will have METR 50% time-horizons in the hundreds of hours which may enable them to play video games well, and will greatly accelerate many areas of research, potentially including video generation research.

We based these video-generation time-horizon estimations on this paper from Google about Veo 2 and Veo 3. The fact that results from both 2 and 3 were provided on certain timehorizny tasks meant that we could calculate the time horizon of each model which means we could calculate a time-horizon doubling-time which means that we could project video generation model time-horizon forward. Error bars are big due to only 2 data points on only 1 task, but extrapolation from few datapoints is what we do.

Naive projection of Veo time-horizons:

  • Veo 2 (dec 2024): 0 seconds
  • Veo 3 (may 2024): 1 second
  • Veo 4 (dec 2025): 8 seconds
  • Veo 5 (may 2026): 64 seconds

This is pretty similar to time-horizon for LLMs. It's about complicated problems. So a Veo-5 agent could wander around indefinitely, but not solve a really hard puzzle which would take a skilled human multiple minutes, e.g. solving an 6x6x6 Rubik's Cube (while it likely could solve a 3x3x3 Rubik's Cube).

The question then becomes how hard is the hardest part of speedrunning Minecraft?. If it's less than a minute or two (remember, the model gets multiple attempts at its run) in length, then the Minecraft market will likely resolve YES. Let's watch a speedrun to see.

Broader Implications

Robotics.

So we'll have a general roboticist capable of completing tasks in the real world of lengths up to a ~minute by EOY 2026, assuming naive extrapolation holds up. A general robot which can do tasks of length up to one minute can be used to automate a lot of things. We'll be getting closer to a robot that can navigate around a house, clean, do laundry, do dishes. These simple tasks as of yet unsolved by general robotics. But most jobs require solving some more difficult tasks e.g. in mining. Certain roles will be automatable though. Remember, this is assuming hyperexponentiality, but hyperexponentiality is a real possibility (and if we extend another year it becomes a certainty).

So, it's like programming productivity will clearly increase massively in 2026, but it's unclear if there will be enough time for this to filter into general AI extreme advances yet by EOY or whether the 50% time horizon of ~100 hours we expect by EOY is sufficient for extremely superhuman developments (like, invent new algorithim that's 1000x more efficient so we get ASI), or is instead only sufficient for a bit of superhumanness.
